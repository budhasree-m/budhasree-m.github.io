// 01 · About

What is the
dark figure of crime?

In criminology, the dark figure refers to all crime that occurs but is never reported to police or recorded in official statistics. In India, this gap is not random — it is structural, shaped by stigma, institutional distrust, caste & gender imbalances, and bureaucratic friction.

T = R / p
T = estimated true incidents  ·  R = NCRB recorded crimes  ·  p = reporting probability
When p < 1, the dark figure D = T − R is non-zero.
In India, p varies widely across crime type, state, and social context.

This project does not claim to know the "true" number — instead, it makes uncertainty visible through uncertainty bands, residual maps, and a composite Visibility Index. Every chart shows assumptions explicitly. No false precision.

// 02 · Visualizations

Two lenses.
One dataset.

The project is structured around two parallel visualization panels — you can view them independently or in direct comparison.

Panel A — Official Record

What NCRB
Tells Us

Recorded crime data as published. State-wise breakdowns, year-over-year trends, IPC category distributions, and charge-sheet rates.

  • Choropleth: crime rate per 1L population
  • Multi-line: IPC category trends 2015–2023
  • Bar chart: state rankings by crime type
  • Stacked bar: charge-sheet & disposal rates
  • Filter: crime type · year · state
Panel B — Dark Figure Lens

What We Might
Be Missing

Statistical models applied to the same data: scenario-based uncertainty bands, regression residuals, and a Visibility Index quadrant.

  • Uncertainty band: recorded vs estimated range
  • Interactive p-slider: adjust reporting probability
  • Residual map: where data defies expectation
  • 4-quadrant scatter: Visibility Index vs recorded crime
  • Annotation layer: auxiliary signals (NFHS-5)

// 03 · Methodology

Three models.
All assumptions explicit.

Model Approach Output Assumption
Scenario Estimation T = R / p, applied at p = 0.3, 0.5, 0.7 Low / mid / high estimate band per state × crime type × year Reporting probability p is uniform within a scenario; does not vary by state
Regression Residuals OLS / Ridge regression: recorded crime ~ literacy + police strength + urbanization + female LFPR + GDP per capita Residual map — states recording far less than predicted flag potential underreporting Socio-demographic predictors are good proxies for expected crime; omitted variables may affect residuals
Visibility Index Normalized composite of literacy, female LFPR, police per lakh, helpline usage 0–1 score per state, used as X-axis in quadrant scatterplot Chosen indicators are reliable proxies for reporting likelihood; equal weighting applied unless otherwise noted

// 04 · Data Sources

Where the numbers
come from

01
NCRB — Crime in India Reports

Primary dataset. Annual reports 2015–2023. State & UT level IPC crime counts, charge-sheet rates, police strength. ncrb.gov.in

02
NFHS-5 (2019–21)

National Family Health Survey. Domestic violence prevalence module used as an empirical anchor for reporting probability estimates in that category.

03
Census 2011 / 2021 Projections

Population denominators for crime rate normalization. Literacy rates, urbanization shares, female labor force participation.

04
MHA Annual Reports & MOSPI

Police strength per lakh population, state-wise economic indicators used as regression predictors.
