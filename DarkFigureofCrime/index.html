// 01 · The Problem

Recorded ≠ Real.
The dark figure is the gap.

In criminology, the dark figure of crime refers to all crime that occurs but is never reported to police or recorded in official statistics. In India, this gap is not random — it is structural, shaped by stigma, institutional distrust, caste and gender power imbalances, and bureaucratic friction.

For a crime to appear in NCRB data it must pass two filters: someone must report it, and police must register it. If either fails, it never becomes a number. What we visualize here is both the number — and the filter.

T = R ÷ p
T = estimated true crime incidents
R = NCRB recorded (official) crimes
p = reporting probability (0 → 1)
D = T − R = the dark figure

At p=0.3 in 2023: T ≈ 12.5M · R = 3.76M · D ≈ 8.8M

// 02 · Methodology

Three models.
All assumptions explicit.

This project does not claim to know the true number. It makes uncertainty visible through three complementary statistical lenses — each with stated assumptions, no false precision.

Model 01 · Scenario Estimation
T = R / p

Applied at p = 0.3, 0.5, 0.7 simultaneously. An interactive slider lets you explore the full continuum. Produces a plausible range, not a single claimed truth.

Model 02 · Regression Residuals
Actual − Predicted

OLS regression predicts crime rate from log population. States with large negative residuals — recording far less than expected — flag potential underreporting patterns.
