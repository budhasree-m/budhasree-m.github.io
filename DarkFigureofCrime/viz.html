// 03 · Practitioner Workshop · Estimate Your p Panel C · p-Estimator
What this panel does: The dark figure formula T = R/p has one unknown — p, the probability that a crime gets reported. This tool helps you estimate p by weighting a set of structural, institutional, and social variables drawn from criminological literature and the NCRB data. Each variable pushes p up or down. The final estimate is a weighted average — a reasoned position, not a measurement. Contributions from this session are stored in memory and shown in the log below. Nothing is saved across sessions.

Regression Residuals — Where Data Defies Expectation
OLS: crime rate ~ log(population) · residual = actual − predicted
Negative residual (orange) — state records less crime than its population size predicts. Possible underreporting, strong stigma barriers, or low institutional capacity to register FIRs.
Positive residual (green) — records more than predicted. May reflect better reporting infrastructure, genuine crime concentration, or stronger FIR registration culture.
Population is the sole predictor here — a simplified proxy. Residuals are signals for investigation, not proof of underreporting.
// 03 · Methodology & Transparency

Every Assumption
Made Explicit

Model 01
Scenario Estimation

T = R/p applied interactively via slider, and statically at p=0.3/0.5/0.7. Produces a plausible range — not a single truth. p is held constant within each scenario and does not vary by state or crime type.

Model 02
Regression Residuals

OLS: crime rate ~ log(population). Residuals flag states deviating from expected. Future iterations will add literacy, police strength per lakh, female LFPR, and GDP per capita for a proper Visibility Index.

Data Sources
NCRB 2023

National time series 1981–2023 (NCRB "At a Glance") and Table 1A state-wise IPC crimes 2021–2023. Population: NCRB mid-year projected 2023. No NFHS-5 or auxiliary data in this version.

What this project does not claim: These visualizations do not assert a definitive dark figure. They demonstrate that silence in data does not mean absence of crime. All models are transparent, assumptions are stated, and uncertainty is shown — not hidden.
